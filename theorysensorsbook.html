A. Scope. Wireless ad-hoc sensor networks have recently become a very active research subject. However, a solid foundational and theoretical background seems necessary for sensor networks to achieve their full potential. It is a challenge for abstract modelling, algorithmic design and analysis to achieve efficient and fault-tolerant realizations of such very large, highly-dynamic, complex, non-conventional networks.

Several models, algorithms and results have already appeared, in relevant specialized and generic theory conferences. This book aims to reinforce the emergence of a critical mass of theoretical foundations, by bringing together in a systematic way high quality contributions (invited book chapters) by leading experts worldwide related to important algorithmic and complexity-theoretic aspects of wireless sensor networks. The intended audience includes researchers and graduate students working on sensor networks and the broader areas of wireless networking and distributed computing, as well as practitioners in the relevant application areas. The book can also serve as a text for advanced courses and seminars.

B. Thematic structure. The book tentatively consists of the following themes:

T1. Introduction, Technology, Applications and Challenges

T2. Sensor Network Models, Topologies, Connectivity and Coverage

T3. Algorithms for Data Propagation

T4. Geo-routing and Obstacle Avoidance Algorithms

T5. Localization and Tracking

T6. Energy Management and Power Saving Schemes

T7. Distributed Combinatorial Optimization and Approximation Algorithms

T8. Algorithmic Aspects of Time Synchronization

T9. Security and Trust

T10. Mobility Aspects for Algorithmic Design

T11. Information Theory for Sensor Networks

Each theme may include subthemes. In fact, the final structure will emerge as soon as authors provide the first version of their chapters.

E. Format of Chapters.

An indicative length for chapters is 30-35 pages. To avoid reformatting at the final stage, make sure TO NOT USE the usual Springer/LNCS Format, but instead the SVMULT DOCUMENT CLASS, which is the Springer Verlag global LaTeX2e support for multi authored books that can be found here, i.e. use latex file "author.tex". No need to strictly adhere to a "uniform" chapter structure; towards "some degree" of uniformity, authors are encouraged to follow (if suitable in each case) the "usual" structure in algorithmic research papers e.g. introduction-motivation, model, problem, algorithm(s), analysis, conclusions and further research.
